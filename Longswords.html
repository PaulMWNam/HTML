The Longsword

The longsword is a weapon of a ambigious nature, with a storied history. Used to describe a wide variety of different swords, longswords are a two handed weapon historically used in war, dueling and entertainment. They typically consist of four main parts.

  • The Pommel: A counterweight found on one end of the weapon, usually round or pear-shaped in nature. It provides the lever action that a longsword uses to provide strong, powerful cuts.
  • The Grip: The section where a swordsman will typically hold his weapon. The grip is typically 9-11 inches long to allow enough room for a two handed grip. The offhand will usually hold the weapon near the bottom of the grip or on the pommel directly.
  • The Quillon: Often seen as two protrusions that extend horizontally from the base of the blade. The quillons serve to protect the users hands during a duel.
  • The Blade: The "weapon" portion of the longsword, used for fighting one's opponent. They come in a wide variety of lengths ranging from 90 to 110 cm in length and are typically made up of the strong (the wider base) and weak (the tapered end).

Longsword Traditions

While no surviving lineages exist, several manuscripts and treatises have been preserved in history, allowing people to recreate techniques used by swordsmen of the 14-16th century. Masters include:

Fiore dei Liberi of Italian Fencing

Filipo di Vadi of Italian Fencing

Joachim Meyer of German Fencing

Johannes Liechtenauer of German Fencing

The German and Italian schools of fencing share a surprisingly large amount of overlap between the different positions and ways to hold a sword. Some of their techniques also share many similarities but there are a few interesting differences between the two schools.
